  <h4 >Doctors, psychiatric nurses, social workers and community leaders were clear on the urgent need for effective, easily available treatments during their personal introdcutions in the first Vital remote sessions. </h4><h4 >Dispatching from the frontline, they believed psychedelic medicine could be the answer. But they met cost and even time frustrations where available, and prohibition otherwise.</h4><p class="" >“The mental health crisis is real” said a fellow student  earlier this week highlighting a growing volume of patients in desperate need, and a healthcare sector under siege.&nbsp;</p><p class="" >Currently available treatments, including those available at UK ketamine clinics are famously dear. Likewise an Amsterdam session or more exotic retreat. I’m a big believer in psychedelic medicine but for that money I’d prefer a hair transplant. Maybe that’s because I haven’t got major depressive disorder.&nbsp;</p><p class="" >Lots of others do. In particular, the poor. An extensive&nbsp; <a href="https://economics.mit.edu/files/18694" target="_blank">2020 report for MIT Economics</a> cites that, internationally, ‘Rates of depression, anxiety, and suicide correlate negatively with income. Those with the lowest incomes in a community suffer one and a half to three times more frequently from depression, anxiety and other common mental illnesses.’</p><h3 data-rte-preserve-empty="true" ></h3><h3 ><strong>“We see competing, profit-seeking ways of turning psychedelics into something that are going to be <em>less</em> accessible”</strong></h3><p class="sqsrte-small" >Dr Erica Dyck</p><p class="" data-rte-preserve-empty="true" ></p><p class="" >My ADHD meds are more effective than anti-depressants from what I can tell and I’m fortunate to have them. Lots of others don’t – and they’re in prison. The Justice Select Committee Inquiry into Mental Health in Prisons here in the UK were told recently in an <a href="https://committees.parliament.uk/writtenevidence/36362/pdf/" target="_blank">expert witness report</a> ‘An estimated 25% of prisoners have ADHD. Despite the disproportionate prevalence, ADHD remains critically under-diagnosed. Furthermore, there is insufficient commissioning of ADHD care pathways across the system.’ A 2010 <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3016316/">report from Sweden</a> registered 40% only two of whom had received diagnoses as children and went for a figure of ’40% of adult male longer-term prison inmates. Further, ADHD and coexisting disorders, such as substance use disorder, anti-social behaviour, personality disorders plus mood and anxiety disorders, severely affected prison inmates with ADHD. Nearly 80% of adults with ADHD, present with at least one coexisting psychiatric disorder.’</p><p class="" >Depression and anxiety are indeed more achievable diagnoses, here in the UK for adults at least. When my ADHD was identified after the best part of fifty years, I was first prescribed Elvanse after a rigorous process by a noted expert, at significant expense. My National Health Service GP refused to take on the ‘shared care agreement’ that provides a subsidised prescription, and told me that the surgery he runs doesn’t deal with it at all.&nbsp;</p><p class="" data-rte-preserve-empty="true" ></p><h3 >‘Primitive psychology has produced no methods for solving crime, conflict, alienation, prejudice, stupidity, boredom, aggression, unhappiness…’</h3><p class="sqsrte-small" >Dr Timothy Leary</p><p class="" data-rte-preserve-empty="true" ></p><p class="" >Fortunately I found another doctor. Or I wouldn’t be able to afford it. Even though it’s essentially speed. While we’re on the subject my psychiatrist told me <a href="https://www.medicines.org.uk/emc/product/10977/smpc#gref" target="_blank">ketamine treatment</a> was “expensive”, the only time I’ve heard him use the word.&nbsp;</p><p class="" >Vital’s lecturer this week was Canadian historian <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nlkYY962014" target="_blank">Erica Dyck</a>. Among her specialities is the early research of Abram Hoffer and Humphry Osmond, the British scientist who turned on Aldous Huxley and coined the term ‘psychedelic’. During the Q&amp;A I asked her what she learned about human nature through her research.</p><p class="" >“The early researchers definitely were trying to align a health access point within a publicly funded system,” she responded, “That is certainly not on the horizon today. We see lots of competing, profit-seeking ways of turning psychedelics into something that, I would argue, are going to be <em>less</em> accessible.”</p><p class="" data-rte-preserve-empty="true" ></p><h3 ><strong>‘Keeping ADHD prisoners in jail costs the UK £74 million, but it’d only cost £30,000 to treat them’</strong></h3><p class="sqsrte-small" >Dr Phil Anderton, police oficer turned therapist</p><p class="" data-rte-preserve-empty="true" ></p><p class="" >Consciousness is policed by an old boys’ club with a mighty pen (if famously shit handwriting). The medical consensus-cum-caper fills its boots from patented versions of otherwise cheap chemicals, or narrative-approved, victim-blaming re-education sessions. Both of which continue to make it wealthy, at the considerable expense of the less fortunate.&nbsp;And the taxpayer. Keeping ADHD prisoners in jail costs the UK £74 million, but it’d <a href="https://www.adhdawarenessmonth.org/adhd-in-prison-population/" target="_blank">only cost £30,000</a> to treat them.</p><p class="" >Dr Leary, who was once on a path to being the Sigmund Freud of his generation, in <em>Exo-Psychology</em>: </p><p class="" >“Primitive psychology, in spite of its enormous, state-supported bureaucracy and its priesthood mystique, has produced no verifiable theory for explaining human behaviour, nor any methods for solving the classic problems of human society: crime, conflict, alienation, prejudice, stupidity, boredom, aggression, unhappiness, and philosophic ignorance about the meaning of life.”</p><p class="" >Yet plant medicines remain forbidden, exclusive, or gate-kept by bureaucracy, ignorance, banality and greed.</p><p class="" >Even though they grow in the garden. And the healthcare workers we claim to value are insistent they can help address this mental health crisis, which is real.</p>
