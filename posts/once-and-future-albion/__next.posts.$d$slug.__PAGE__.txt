<h2>“It’s a hopeful, optimistic interpretation… blended, dynamic, fantastical,” says British artist Jeremy Deller of his vivid cartographic <em>A Dream of Britain</em> pictured above. </h2>
<p>The vibrant painting of the United Kingdom closed the British Museum’s smash Stonehenge show of spring/summer 2022. Deller is colourblind so sees it differently to you and I. This he says reflects a national identity that is forever intersubjective, and in flux.</p>
<p>In <a href="/home-1-1-1" target="">issue one of the Vital Student Zine</a> I <a href="https://kool-aid-corner-1-yjbya">pulled out <em>Albion Dreaming</em></a><em>,</em> Andy Roberts’ history of <a href="https://bureauoflostculture.podbean.com/e/albion-dreaming-a-brief-trip-through-the-history-of-british-lsd/">psychedelic Britain</a>: “From the Sixties onwards sections of the counter culture used the term Albion to refer to their vision of a land, society and individual consciousness based on the insights offered by LSD,” writes Roberts of the romantic goal gathering pace in pop culture.</p>
<p>All the big gun historians from Pliny the Elder, through Marinus of Tyre, to Geoffrey of Monmouth cite Albion as the original name for these sceptred (in a non-dual way) isles. The legend is kick-ass (arse). First, the original King of Syria, or a King of somewhere in Greece maybe, had 43 (or possibly 33) illegitimate daughters who all got married on the same day, killed their husbands on the same night, and fled right here. Where there were no men. So they had it off with incubi – the male equivalent of a saucy ‘succubus’ sex demon – and produced a race of giants. The sisters named the place after their alpha female, Alba.</p>
</div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 2346px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.tate.org.uk/whats-on/tate-britain/william-blake-artist"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/William_Blake_-_Albion_Rose_-_from_A_Large_Book_of_Designs_1793-6-scaled.avif?format=original" alt="    William Blake     ’s ‘The Dance of Albion'   "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">William Blake     ’s ‘The Dance of Albion’</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<p>In the sequel, beleaguered Brutus of Troy is fleeing his eponymous horse fiasco when the freaking <em>goddess Diana</em> appears and tells him to voyage beyond Gaul to “raise a second Troy”. There were only 24 giants left by then including the fearsome Gogmagog, who got chucked off the white cliffs of Dover. That’s my GoT-style swashbuckling version with apols to Geoffrey of Monmouth and the crew.</p>
<h3>“To the counter culture Albion refers to a land, society and individual consciousness based on the insights offered by LSD”</h3>
<p>Any sort of ‘New Albion’ got off to an auspicious start after Sir Francis Drake used it as the first name for California upon landing in 1579. Since then it’s had further ups-and-downs. Esoteric saint, the poet and painter, William Blake named Albion ‘the primeval titan’ from which his four aspects of man sprang, and pictured it as a giant wearing nothing but a broad grin against a rural Utopian background featuring splashes of pastel colour… <em>apropos</em> of nothing. </p>
<p>Blake invokes Albion when in need of a term to idealise Britain: his <em>Vision of the Daughters of Albion</em> is a feminist protest poem influenced by his friend, and collaborator, Mary Wolstencraft Shelle. And he cries “does this thing happen on Albion’s shores?” in <em>Little Boy Lost,</em> an ode against child cruelty. The English cricket, rugby, and Commonwealth Games athletic teams all use Blake’s proto-socialist hymn <em>Jerusalem</em> instead of the official national anthem.</p>
</div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 2250px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://thealbionrooms.live"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Albion-Rooms-2-Bedrooms-by-Jason-Knott.avif?format=original" alt="   Pete Doherty’s Margate HQ      The Albion Rooms    "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Pete Doherty’s Margate HQ      The Albion Rooms</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<p>Shadow side ‘Perfidious Albion’ was a term invented by French bishops to bemoan England’s Dark Ages clerical set-up. Later, French Revolutionaries assumed support from the country that toppled its monarchy and installed a puppet aristocracy a century previous. When it didn’t come, the former <em>miserables</em> ran with the term propagandising an, erm, supposed track record of diplomatic betrayal, even bringing up the whole Joan of Arc thing <em>again </em>which everyone knows they were in on.</p>
</div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.marksheeky.com/painting.php?idnumber=0714004"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/CHE_CGM_2010_21-001.avif?format=original" alt="   ‘Two Roman Legionaries Discovering The God-King Albion Turned Into Stone’ by Mark Sheeky   "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">‘Two Roman Legionaries Discovering The God-King Albion Turned Into Stone’ by Mark Sheeky</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<p><em>Two Roman Legionaries Discovering The God-King Albion Turned Into Stone</em> is a <a href="https://www.marksheeky.com/painting.php?idnumber=0714004">2008 painting</a> by Mark Sheeky. He says, “It’s inspired by Brief Encounter, a film from 1949 that showed a Britain which no longer exists, a country and time so alien to the Britain of 2008 that it is difficult to believe that a place like that ever existed. I wanted to represent the end of that old Britain, so I chose the end of another era as the setting. Two foreigners, Roman legionaries, walk towards the edge of Britain in the grey rain. Through mud, to the grassy limit of the country, the top of the great white cliffs. As they reach the edge they discover a giant stone man standing in the sea, the once king Albion, now dead and grey and cracked. A statue preserved like a memory. A reminder of an ancient time now gone forever.”</p>
<p>Or has it? Under re-story-ation rules fiction can be considered as powerful as the imagined past, right? And in terms of syncronicitous relevance, the phrase Albion cropping up like this must be some sort of sign?</p>
<h3>“I’m not an activist, I’m a fantasist”</h3>
<p>Here in C21 the Dionysus figure of our second Atlantis, musician Pete Doherty evokes Albion so strenuously that he’s opened a hotel in Margate (it’s like Portland crossed with Oakland, by the sea) called <a href="https://thealbionrooms.live">The Albion Rooms</a>. “Reebok classics, and canons at dawn; terrible warlords, good warlords, and an English song” goes Doherty’s band Babyshambles’ gentle rabble-rouser <em>Down in Albion</em>. “I’m not an activist, I’m a fantasist. Inverted snobbery is just as dangerous as snobbery itself, you know – that pride in having nothing,” he says.</p>
</div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 3000px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.albionbarn.com/albion-fields/"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/erwin-wurm.avif?format=original" alt="   By Erwin Wurm at      Albion Fields      sculpture park   "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">By Erwin Wurm at      Albion Fields      sculpture park</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<p>Over at the other end of the quantum funnel from this grass-roots desire for a new national identity lies <a href="https://www.albionbarn.com/albion-fields/">Albion Fields</a> sculpture park, open till end of October 2022. It’s an outdoor exhibition free to anyone but to which nonetheless ‘the glitterati are flocking’ <a href="https://www.tatler.com/article/albion-fields-sculpture-park">according to Tatler</a> magazine. </p>
<p>The woodland’s owner (in fact it was planted at his birth) is Michael Hue-Williams, an art dealer who first showed Ai Weiwei in the UK and represents generation-defining photographer <a href="https://www.albionbarn.com/exhibitions/11-nick-knight-roses/cover/">Nick Knight</a>. He says, ‘Walking through these beautiful grounds during lockdown, I realised I have a unique opportunity to share the experience.’ Perhaps reciprocity can exist at all levels. Once and future.</p>
