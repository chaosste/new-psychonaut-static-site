<h3><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #ED8CAD">“Biology drives the effects of psychedelics but therapy shapes them,” says the latest scion in the Nichols psycho-pharmacological dynasty</span></h3>
</div></div></div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1162px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://lucilehaute.fr"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/rabbit-grin-ron-portrait.avif?format=original" alt="     Ron English,      ‘Rabbit Grin’    "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Ron English,      ‘Rabbit Grin’</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<h2>The freshly ‘neuroplastic’ brain and new grey matter created during ‘neurogenesis’ both require careful curation from therapy afterwards, declares Dr Charles Nichols.</h2>
<p>It’s notable that a hardcore neuroscientist stresses the importance of combining his drugs with talk therapy. </p>
<p>“If you don’t have therapy in the weeks after you may go back to that baseline state,” says the <a href="https://www.5ht2a.org">star chemist</a>, “the process strengthens newly made connections and dampens old ones.”</p>
<p>It’s a clear decision he’s come to after a career formally studying the effects of mind-altering chemicals, under exhaustive laboratory conditions. And taking fatherly advice from dad David, the most prolific psychedelic chemist of his generation. </p>
<p>‘Neuroplastic’ effects last for many days after the psychedelic experience itself. Little spiky nodules sticking out from the surface of brain cells called ‘dendrites’ grow in cells all over the brain. This provides fertile ground for fresher, healthier thinking patterns to germinate and grow. </p>
<p>‘Neurogenesis’ is different. It’s the generation of <em>new</em> brain cells. Those ones your school nurse said you’d never get back. Admittedly establishment science is yet to entirely admit she was be wrong. Humans are only capable of neurogenesis in the hippocampus, boffins reckon. We get it from aerobic exercise, sex, worthwhile achievement and all the other good stuff.</p>
<p>No prizes whatsoever for guessing what else is said to cause neurogenesis. </p>
<p>Say neurogenesis is real and not some figment of the ever-lively psychedelic imagination. Given it definitely happens in chimps and rats it probably is. These new brain cells require injecting with healthy thought patterns by integration tactics and therapy too.</p>
<p>What’s more, Dr Charles Nichols, born of David, categorically states that psilocybin is a more effective anti-depressant treatment than ketamine.</p>
<h2>“If you don’t have therapy in the weeks after you may go back to that baseline state”</h2>
<p>Although ketamine boasts impressive effects including its distinct ‘glutamate surge’ and anti-microbial properties, Charles’ rats felt psilocybin’s anti-depressant powers for much longer.</p>
<p>Real psychedelics use their own neuropathic pathway to create neuroplasticity, believes Charles, not the MTOR pathway usually associated with glutamate-derived GABA and any ketamine-led ‘surge’ thereof. </p>
<p>Charles’ lab rats are still above their baseline satisfaction scores three months into the official testing period and counting. On ketamine they were back to baseline after one week. “Both will snap back but the difference is significant,” comments Charles.</p>
