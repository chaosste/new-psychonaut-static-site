<h4><strong>My unofficial <em>Vital Study Zine </em>#<em>16</em> with observations from Vital Psychedelic Training and recent happenings in the space</strong></h4>
</div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.pamelasimard.com"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/pamela-simard-portrait.avif?format=original" alt="      Pamela Simard     , ‘BDL’   "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Pamela Simard     , ‘BDL’</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<h2>Dr Charles Nichols – son of canonical chemist <a href="https://psychedelicstoday.com/2019/08/06/dr-david-nichols-a-rich-and-historical-overview-of-psychedelic-chemistry/">Dr David Nichols</a> – puts forward a single very good reason for extracting the profound components from psychedelics. </h2>
<p>“These drugs are taking so long to develop because the FDA wants much more rigorous testing,” he informs us from the front line of medical authorisation. </p>
<p>One particular medicine on <a href="https://www.5ht2a.org">his to-do list</a> Charles found by isolating the properties of mescaline, synthesised from the peyote cactus. </p>
<p>Turns out psychedelics interact with cells in the soft muscle tissue <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6748996/">around the heart</a>, as well as the brain. He’s already registered a <a href="https://patents.justia.com/inventor/charles-d-nichols">patent</a>.</p>
<p>While LSD for example “isn’t a very strong anti-inflammatory” Charles says, mescaline has an <a href="https://jpet.aspetjournals.org/content/327/2/316">“extremely potent”</a> effect on inflammatory-based issues like, for example, breathing condition <a href="https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsptsci.0c00063">asthma</a>. </p>
<p>The discovery could have major implications for psychedelic healing of previously unconsidered issues. Not just physical struggles like asthma but also <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/257464105_Serotonin_5-HT2A_Receptor_Activation_Blocks_TNF-a_Mediated_Inflammation_In_Vivo">schizophrenia, Parkinson’s and Alzheimer’s which are all connected with inflammation</a>.</p>
<p>“So much has to do with inflammation and the over-active immune system right now,” says Charles, pharmacology professor at LSU Health Sciences in New Orleans with a background at Purdue and Vanderbilt universities.</p>
</div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.5ht2a.org"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/cnichols-Enhanced.avif?format=original" alt="    Charles on the      job     . Embroidered lab coats FTW   "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Charles on the      job     . Embroidered lab coats FTW</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<p>‘Inflammation’ is shorthand here for ‘chronic inflammation’. Oxidants produced in the aftermath of a stress hormone spike for example, linger around and screw stuff up over time.</p>
<h2>“I never intended to follow my father into psychedelics actually”</h2>
<p>It’s a cause of cancer, psoriasis, arthritis, asthma, allergies, Chron’s Disease, hepatitis, neurodegenerative diseases like Parkinson’s, and more. In younger folks chronic inflammation is mostly caused by stress, bad diets and poor sleep. </p>
<p>The chemical aspect in question has nothing to do with the ‘psychedelic’ properties of mescaline. It has the same anti-asthmatic effect on its own <em>sans</em> tripping when tested on Charles’ elite lab rats bred for psychometric testing. </p>
<p>The intrepid rodents tested it out against 25 other designer psychedelics from the drug cupboard Charles inherited from his father David: the pharmacologist who synthesised DMT for <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-7">Rick Strassman</a>, <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-12">MDMA for MAPS</a> and <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-11">psilocybin for PsiloDep 2</a>.</p>
<p>“I never intended to follow my father into psychedelics actually,” says Charles coyly when I ask in the Q&amp;A if he’d ever noticed the hand of fate guiding his work – like it did when Albert Hoffman felt compelled to re-examine the LSD that’d sat on his shelf un-investigated for seven years.</p>
<p>Charles tried to evade his cosmic destiny in vain. Two separate freaky coincidences nudged him towards a career in consciousness expansion. </p>
<p>The eventual pharmacologist initially studied genetics. Even now Charles’ ‘animal models’, creatures bred for testing purposes, are much envied in scientific circles. </p>
<p>Exhausted by the minutiae of fruit fly genetics after finishing a Phd, the younger Charles was restless for change. Twirling absent-mindedly on his lab stool wondering how to enter the world of employment, Charles spied a promo ad for a new book from Vanderbilt University scientist Elaine Sanders-Bush, who he’d heard his father mention. </p>
<p>He called up and asked about assistant roles. </p>
<p>“It turned out the job involved studying the effects of LSD on mouse and rat brains,” says Charles, “it was one of only a handful of labs doing so at the time. They’d run out of budget for now and couldn’t hire anyone. But a few months later Elaine called and asked if I was interested.”</p>
<p>Charles met Sanders and showed her his resumé featuring his education at Perdue University. “Elaine said she knew a Dr David Nichols there, at which point I had to tell her,” confesses Charles. </p>
<h2>“Eventually we might isolate the qualities of peak experience, ego dissolution and breaking stuck thinking”</h2>
<p>The next turning point came as Charles was working at New Orleans University in the chaotic aftermath of Hurricane Katrina. Struggling to find researchers he took a chance on a visiting Chinese scientist who needed facilities. By 2013 Charles and collaborator Bangning Yu had isolated the effects of mescaline-derived DOI on <a href="https://jpet.aspetjournals.org/content/327/2/316">inhibiting tumour necrosis factor (TNF)-a mediated inflammation</a>, which is associated with asthma, Parkinson’s Disease and many more conditions not previously part of psychedelic research.</p>
<p>‘Our data suggests,’ reads the modest blurb, ‘that sub-behavioural levels of certain psychedelics represents a new, steroid-sparing, small molecule strategy for the treatment of peripheral inflammatory related diseases.’ </p>
<p>Targeting diseases on the fringe of psychedelic potential is not where Charles’ quietly vast ambition ends, though. </p>
<p>The basic neuro-scientific explanation of how psychedelics do their thing is the chemicals interact with receptors in the body’s cells. In particular ones given the code ‘5-HT2a’ usually given over to the neurotransmitter serotonin. </p>
<p>LSD contains serotonin in its chemical make-up. It latches on to receptors; hence the lengthy trip. Serotonin certainly isn’t simply ‘the happiness chemical’. It’s related to memory, learning, imagination, sexual arousal, appetite, anxiety and ‘diseases with complex etiologies.’</p>
<p>“Pressing different 5-HT2a receptors, and others, creates the various effects,” says Charles. Identifying medical properties could be just the beginning, he insists: “Eventually we might isolate the qualities of peak experience, ego dissolution and breaking stuck thinking.”</p>
<p>That’s not all. A new testing system Charles has designed examines the long terms effects of a single dose, identifying ‘persistent normalisation of stress-induced hippocampal dysfunction relevant to depression and other psychiatric conditions’</p>
