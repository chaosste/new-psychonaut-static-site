<h2>Thought integrating ‘the parts of you that you’re most ashamed of and keep hidden’ was the trickiest part of Jungian shadow work?</h2>
<p>You may be unsurprised to learn that the cycles are just beginning.</p>
<p>There’s the ‘golden shadow’. </p>
<p>This includes the silver linings that inevitably accompany the regular shadow’s negative traits. </p>
<p>Plus perhaps more importantly the <em>agreeable and positive aspects</em> that we’ve smothered. We might suppress compassionate urges to appear indifferent (hard and cool in other words).</p>
<h2>“We take the collective shadow and make it our own”</h2>
<p>Sounds reasonably clear-cut. </p>
<p>Yet be wary of discriminating between the golden shadow and the murky, original one. “The ‘positive vibes only’ culture is anti-shadow,” says says Jungian analyst Dr Ido Cohen, lecturing in <a href="https://www.vitalpsychedelictraining.com">Vital’s</a> shadow work lecture series, “what might be happening that oppresses the shadow?”</p>
<p>That’ll be ‘the collective shadow’. It’s the one who also nailed Christ to the cross, and willingly elects dictators. “We take the collective shadow and make it our own,” <a href="https://www.instagram.com/dr.idocohen/">Dr  Cohen</a> expands. </p>
<p>“We can absorb the collective shadow, or become numb to it,” adds Dr Portal. Likewise we might celebrate our outsider status; just as psychonaut heads like to do. Should we attempt union? Or consolidate independence? </p>
<p>“What does integration look like?” says Dr Ido Cohen, “Moving away from these systems and creating something new? Or is it taking pieces of the experience and carrying forth?”</p>
<p>British christian ecologist Paul Kingsnorth has a stark view on navigating shadow work. ‘Sacrifice and surrender are at the heart of faiths,’ he points out, ‘Nobody wants this. But maybe it’s what we have to do?’</p>
