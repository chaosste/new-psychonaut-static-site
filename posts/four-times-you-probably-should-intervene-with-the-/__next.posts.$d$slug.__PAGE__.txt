<h3><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #ED8CAD">Frontline clinical psychologist answers the big question: when the patient’s tripping balls, when does the therapist definitely need to get involved?</span></h3>
</div></div></div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.instagram.com/theorderofthefool/"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/therapy-portrait-Screenshot-2022-11-01-at-10.avif?format=original" alt="     From      The Order of the Fool      Street Tarot exhibition in Manchester    "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">From      The Order of the Fool      Street Tarot exhibition in Manchester</figcaption></figure> </p>

<h1>‘This level of reality should be the new standard being applied to therapy in general’ says a Vital cohort colleague in praise of Dr Lafrance’s theoretically informed approach.</h1>
<p>“It’s different to how we were trained!” replied the respected frontline clinical psychologist. Like you can read about in the <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-17/the-feeling-theory">Approach</a> section this issue, she combines emotion-focussed therapy with a ‘theoretically informed’ style taking cues from lived experiences received in the treatment rooms.</p>
<p>This not only contrasts with bureaucratic doctrine, that favours centralised top-down wisdom… which can take decades to catch up with what’s happening on the ground.</p>
<p>It’s also at odds the non-directive or ‘inner directed’ approach pioneered by Dr Stanislav Grof (for it is he) which insists on more delicate interaction with the therapist during the psychedelic experience itself, to encourage a more detailed internal dialogue. </p>
<h2>“I do warn them in advance that they’ll be more aware than I am”</h2>
<p>That’s the the way psychedelic therapy is going though, Dr Lafrance claims.</p>
<p>Those of us who were only just getting the hang of being non-directive may be left a-flutter by this sudden change in er, direction. So Dr Lafrance has kindly detailed the when’s and why’s of sticking your oar in during the voyager’s experience. </p>
<p>“We still intervene in a relational, emotion-focused, and inner-directed way,” she comments in her <a href="https://www.vitalpsychedelictraining.com">Vital</a> lecture profiling the emotion-focused, theoretically informed style. </p>
<p>Dr Lafrance’s reasons for chipping in? Conflict with parts of the self. Maladaptive emotions like anxiety and self-criticism. Anticipated shame and regret, along the lines of ‘I can’t believe I told you that, it’s supposed to be a secret.’ And, reluctance. </p>
<p>Each must be handled gingerly, and always in the context of the medicine experience – hence ‘inner directed’ still, prompting the patient to look inside themselves, still. </p>
<p>To confirm if your planned intervention is indeed worthwhile, confer with the handy acronym WAIT – ‘why am I talking?’</p>
<h2>“We make a deal in the container that I’m always fine”</h2>
<p>For instance: when confronted by the thought of a sober therapist and spangled patient, an old tripping pal of mine asked, “What happens when they realise <em>they’re</em> taking care of <em>you</em>?”</p>
<p>He was offering up a psychedelic riddle-cum-truism. But it does happen according to Dr Lafrance albeit more prosaically. </p>
<p>“I do warn them in advance that they’ll be more aware than I am,” says Dr Lafrance, “We make a deal in the container [agreed-upon boundaries of behaviour while tripping] that I’m always fine.” Because when patients suddenly start insisting that you go outside for a smoke, grab a coffee, or take some time to yourself “it can be reassuring to just say that you are” says Dr Lafrance.</p>
<p>“But it can be more rewarding to go into that place,” she advises, “is there a process of caretaking that needs looking into?’ Tell them that you’re always comfortable, they don’t need to worry if you’re hungry because it’s been four hours.” </p>
<p>Otherwise, go get that brew now “Or it may feel like a violation.”</p>
<p>Uncharacteristic behaviour can be examined by gently pointing out that “It’s so wonderful to get to know this part of you… feels like it’s a part that really need to show itself?”</p>
<p>That attentive, experience-led vibe seems to be sweet spot of ‘theoretically-informed, inner-led, emotion-focussed therapy.’</p>
