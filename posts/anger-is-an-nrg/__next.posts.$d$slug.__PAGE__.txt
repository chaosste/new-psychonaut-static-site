<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://fluorescentsmogg.com/product/retrato/"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/portrait-body-adam-retrato-body-Enhanced.avif?format=original" alt="    Adam Neate, ‘Retrato’ available from      Flurorescent Smogg    "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">Adam Neate, ‘Retrato’ available from      Flurorescent Smogg</figcaption></figure> </p>

<h2>The reconstructed self doesn’t take any crap. </h2>
<p>“Assertion is a common after-effect of medicine work,” says Dr Lafrance coyly, in her eye-opening lecture to <a href="https://www.vitalpsychedelictraining.com">Vital</a> students on hw psychedelic therapy goes down in the real.</p>
<p>Like the article says over in the <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-17/the-feeling-theory">Approach</a> section of <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-17"><em>Zine #17</em></a> here, Dr Lafrance says therapists see two secondary emotions most commonly in the field.</p>
<h2>“Empowerment skills, when they’ve perhaps not been done before, are usually too mousey… or too much”</h2>
<p>Those two feelings she hears about the most are sadness, as one might expect from the depressed, and rage.</p>
<p>“Anger is a challenge for many people,” says Dr Lafrance, “but not all anger is destructive. There is healthy anger.”</p>
<p>This more righteous kind of fury can often be confused, shall we say, with assertiveness. </p>
<p>“Culturally we really struggle,” comments Dr Lafrance, “I’ve been in therapy for 20 years working on my capacity for anger, standing up for myself, and asking what I need.”</p>
<p>The rage-fuelled are shying away from vulnerability according to emotion focussed therapy, and it “shows up as these problematic reactions that fuel expression of symptoms,” says the clinical psychologist, somewhat eupehmistically.</p>
<p>“Empowerment skills, when they’ve perhaps not been done before, are usually too ‘mousey’, or too much,” explains the blonde boffin, “plus the most predictable reaction to unexpected criticism is defensiveness.”</p>
<p>Dr Lafrance provides scripted frameworks for the budding bearish buccaneer. </p>
<h2>“I always assume user error. You know, because it helps me to cultivate more sophisticated skill”</h2>
<p>She’s kind enough to share one with Vital students during the post-lecture Q&amp;A, when I ask about help with my own fermenting… assertiveness.</p>
<p>“I always assume user error. You know, because it helps me to cultivate more sophisticated skill,” she advises. </p>
<p>“If I’m expressing assertion, especially if it’s in a relationship where it’s kind of a novel experience, and it doesn’t go well… then I ask myself: ‘What am I doing or not doing to contribute to this problem?’” </p>
<p>That’s how she hit on the idea of detailled advice for pateints fumbling their way into self-confidence. </p>
<p>“I was encouraging people to express assertion, and it’s not going well. So then I, as a therapist, asked myself ‘How did I contribute to that?’ Like, ‘Oh, shit, we didn’t warn them.’ So I wrote, I wrote the script now that I give to all the clients.”</p>
<p>And it goes a little something like this:</p>
<p>‘I realised that I don’t always say or tell the truth about how I feel. Or about what I need. It’s hurting me. </p>
<p>And I realise that makes it so that I’m not always honest in relationships. Because I’m scared that I’ll lose people important to me. Including you.</p>
<h2>“Not all anger is destructive. There is healthy anger”</h2>
<p>I really want to make a change in this way. I want to be more honest. And I want to have more faith. </p>
<p>Faith that heart-centered motivation is there at the forefront. And faith that this relationship can sustain the changes that are required for it to evolve. </p>
<p>Would it be okay, if I started, in this relationship, being more honest about the things that hurt? </p>
<p>Or the things that anger me? Knowing that it’s really because I want to find a new normal that will serve us <em>both</em>?’</p>
<p>You might as well give it a go, you’ve tried evrything else. Could avoid an ‘Iatrogenic’ – therapist-induced – divorce.</p>
