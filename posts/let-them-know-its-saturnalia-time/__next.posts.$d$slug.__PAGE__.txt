<h2><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #82ACDC"><strong>Heal</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #A46ECF"><strong> </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: hsl(274, 45%, 52%)"><strong>the</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #7B1DC8"><strong> </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: hsl(280, 70%, 48%)"><strong>world</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: hsl(274, 81%, 35%)"><strong> </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #7B1DC8"><strong>from </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #530DC4"><strong>collective</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #7B1DC8"><strong> </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #571CD9"><strong>trauma</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #7B1DC8"><strong> </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #2A1EDD"><strong>says</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #4F46DC"><strong> </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #928AEB"><strong>Thomas </strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--accent"><strong>Hubl</strong></span><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #7B1DC8"><strong> </strong></span></h2>
</div></div></div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://medicinefestival.com"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/medicine-festival-Enhanced.avif?format=original" alt="   2022’s      Medicine Festival      in the UK   "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">2022’s      Medicine Festival      in the UK</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<p>My journalism network brother Matt Green is the author of <a href="https://www.amazon.co.uk/Aftershock-Fighting-Surviving-Trauma-Finding/dp/1846273315"><em>Aftershock: fighting War, Surviving Trauma and Finding Peace</em></a><em> (New Statesman: ‘Outstanding’</em>, <em>Spectator</em>: ‘A work of integrity and substance’, TV’s Bear Grylls: <em>‘Compelling, humbling and inspiring’</em>). </p>
<p>He examines collective trauma in his new blog <a href="https://matthewgreenglobal.substack.com/"><em>Resonant World</em></a> – and ways to heal it. Like the UK’s <a href="https://medicinefestival.com">Medicine Festival</a> whereupon he has hence returned. </p>
<p>“I’m going to sound too idealistic and starry-eyed about what is basically a fun gathering in a field,” Matt reports, “But a core part of me knows I came away feeling more peaceful, grounded and inspired than when I arrived — and I’ve learned to trust that felt-sense more than my fear of sounding naive.”</p>
<p>Matt, who’s on the environment beat for Reuters right now, worked as a war correspondent in Iraq and Afghanistan. He spoke on the launch address call for <a href="https://collectivetraumasummit.com/">The Collective Trauma Summit 2022</a> (oh yes), cooked up by guru Thomas Hubl author of <em>Healing Collective Trauma</em>. Which features <a href="https://collectivetraumasummit.com/">basically everyone</a> from the wonderful world of 21st century wellbeing. </p>
<p>“Trauma on a personal level is energy, on a collective level it’s a storm,” says Hubl, who talks about activating our ‘collective immune system.’ He maintains “it’s an important function of collective health and if we don’t even have it, that’s a sign of our health.”</p>
<p>Matt addressed journalism’s part in mankind’s burnout. “The media is frozen, reporting what’s ‘out there’ as if it’s on a glass screen, stuck in a psychic sludge,” says the father of two married to a children’s therapist, “we’re ‘looking at broken glass through broken glass’ to borrow Thomas’ own phrase.”</p>
<p>Us media scum need unconditional loving too, says Matt: “That’s true of journalists as much as the rest of us who were born into this traumatised society. Journalists are recognising they need to be healing themselves as individuals, having perhaps been part of environments that encourage trauma-causing behaviours. Clinging to the notion that objectivity could protect us, was a fiction.”</p>
<p>When <em>Sunday Times</em> suits wouldn’t sign off heroic war reporter Marie Colvin’s expenses, hacks left a cow’s eyeball on the accounts desk in reference to the eye Colvin lost to a rocket propelled grenade blast while covering the Sri Lankan civil war in 2001 (it gave her PTSD). She sported her signature eye patch thereafter. </p>
<p>Driven on by her own unique quest for homeostasis, Colvin died reporting under news blackout from the siege of Homs in 2012 when her building was hit by Syrian artillery.</p>
<p>“Journalism can be a form of healing too though,” says Matt.</p>
<p>Decorated foreign correspondent <a href="https://www.reuters.com/investigates/special-report/ptsd-witness-yates/">Dean Yates was shattered by PTSD</a> after two of his team were killed by an Apache gunship crew. Wikileaks dug up pilot cam footage that made for difficult viewing. Yates beat himself up further for not making enough of a storm with it. Eventually he was admitted to a psychiatric ward. </p>
<p>Now, Yates talks about his experience at edgy institutions. He and Reuters set up a blog site and mini-community where burnt-out broadcasters and wobbly world-slingers could exhume a bit of trauma by banging out some posts. </p>
<p>“Slowly the culture started to change a little,” says Matt, “I’m not saying there isn’t a long way to go for the media…”</p>
<p>My cousin’s in Ukraine right now with the BBC. The most traumatic my journalism career ever got was when I reviewed Ian Schraeger’s new hotel and the remote for the TV in the suite didn’t work. No it wasn’t. It was when a close colleague and I were on the wrong end of a corporate ‘moral injury’ and he killed himself. </p>
<p>Not that I, he or anyone in that position was, or is, above such behaviour ourselves. Neither are we discouraged to be, like Matt points out. And we’re the lucky ones. “None of this is possible without a community system that offers support, equity and justice,” points out Matt’s fellow host on the Collective Trauma summit, heavyweight sociologist <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8an9pn-MeAk&amp;list=PLHkFG2EjLJZGnFWGnHNezWXAieDAJf1Hj&amp;index=6">Dr Ruby Mendenhall</a>. Her work highlights the need to address racism as a health crisis given its eventual, detrimental effects on health, lives and budgets.</p>
<p>“Our own experience of trauma is not deep enough to feel deeper traumas holistically,” says Hubl of social engineering, “We provide properly; we see them as problems and patch them up with ideas. Yet there is an intensity of emotions we are not able to address.” Mendenhall’s work with communities in Illinois includes community wellbeing centres like those in <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-8/village-green-frome-model-healthcare">Compassionate Care Frome</a>, plus business incubators, career roadmaps and personal financial advice. </p>
<p>When not kicking ass, Medenhall dabbles in poetry. Jotting down some rhymes has sort-of neuroscientific healing properties according to the summit’s <a href="https://terapiaparamexico.com/home/">Dr Laura Calderón de la Barca</a>: “It’s using imagination as a container for healing. Beyond words alone, elements from the future and the past can meet.” Careful or I shall be forced to publish my Burroughs-style <a href="https://www.writing.upenn.edu/~afilreis/88v/burroughs-cutup.html">cut-ups</a>.</p>
