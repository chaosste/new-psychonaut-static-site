<h2>Can the inner healer mend a broken relationship?</h2>
<p>Next up for MAPS therapy program designers <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-12/mdma-therapy-michael-annie-mithoefer">Dr Michael Meithofer and his wife Annie</a> AKA ‘Annie and Michael’ in spacespeak, is MDMA for couples’ counselling.</p>
<p>“We knew that MDMA was useful for communication… and some of the other anecdotal things about it,” Annie told none other than Professor David Nutt on the Drug Science podcast (where you can hear Dr Nutt, the David Attenborough of drugs, a UK national treasure say ‘Back to the show!’)</p>
<p>Annie collaborated on the initial <a href="https://Relational%20and%20Growth%20Outcomes%20Following%20Couples%20Therapy%20With%20MDMA%20for%20PTSD%20https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34262496/">research</a> for a new era in <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2022/02/08/well/mind/marriage-molly-mdma.html">MDMA couples’ therapy</a> with Toronto’s <a href="https://psychedelicstoday.com/2021/01/19/pt227/">Dr Anne Wagner</a>. The <a href="https://remedycentre.ca/">Remedy</a> clinic director has come up during further investigations into juicy subjects two weeks in a row (sync). Last week it was in a <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-11/love-unconditional-psychedelics-personality-disorder-bpd">call for further research into psychedelic treatment for borderline personality disorder (BPD)</a>.</p>
<p>Yet Dr Wagner is not the only intrepid sailor of the soul <a href="https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.733456/full">cooking up excellent experiments</a> using ecstasy. John Hopkins’ university neuroscience department, not to be outdone, <a href="https://www.nationalgeographic.com/animals/article/news-octopuses-ecstasy-mdma-drugs-social-science-brains-ethics">gave E to octopuses</a>. They’d <a href="https://hub.jhu.edu/2018/09/21/octopus-ecstasy-study/">noticed</a> ‘that octopuses and humans had nearly identical genomic codes for the transporter that binds the neurotransmitter serotonin to the neuron’s membrane.’</p>
<p>The California double-spot octopus is a solitary creature, barely interacting with others of its kind besides once a year, briefly, for mating. Even then the male uses a <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_8IVRmaJZio&amp;list=PLHkFG2EjLJZGnFWGnHNezWXAieDAJf1Hj&amp;index=7">sex arm</a> and it looks like mid-air refuelling. </p>
<p>Would you believe though, that when researchers put the octopuses ‘in a beaker containing a liquified version of the drug’ according to <a href="https://www.nationalgeographic.com/animals/article/news-octopuses-ecstasy-mdma-drugs-social-science-brains-ethics"><em>National Geographic</em></a>, they exhibited significantly more social behaviour? </p>
<p>‘Particularly telling, said scientist Gul Dolen, was that after being returned to their tanks at Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute in Massachusetts, the octopuses went on to reproduce.’ </p>
<p>During the Q&amp;A after Annie and Michael’s lecture the pair were asked about giving MDMA to animals. After all, dogs are given anti-depressants. When I got my own chance to talk to them, I celebrated group ceremonial use of the ecstasy sacrament in the form of our rave culture then made a bad taste joke about giving MDMA to our pets hadn’t gone nearly as well. Now we know to shove them in a beaker of it.</p>
<p>’At no point did the octopuses ink, which would be a sign of stress,’ Dr Dolan told Nat Geo in response to all of our ethical concerns. </p>
