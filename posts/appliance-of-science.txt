<h3><span class="sqsrte-text-color--custom" style="color: #8A877D">Purists sneer at scientific tinkering. But lab studies showed Dr Nichols how psychedelics heal the body. Could he uncover the secret of profundity too?</span></h3>
</div></div></div>
<p>       <figure style="width: 1200px" class="wp-caption alignnone"><a href="https://www.ryoichikurokawa.com"><img decoding="async" src="https://newpsychonaut.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/Ryoichi-Kurokawa-future-shock-Enhanced.avif?format=original" alt="     By      Ryoichi Kurokawa    "></a><figcaption class="wp-caption-text">By      Ryoichi Kurokawa</figcaption></figure> </p>
<div class="sqs-html-content" data-sqsp-text-block-content>
<h2>Neuroscience is different to other aspects of psychedelic study, ‘since it is so spectacularly and usefully right over so many things.’</h2>
<p>Raymond Tallis wrote those semi-satirical words in <a href="https://www.abebooks.co.uk/Aping-Mankind-Neuromania-Darwinitis-Misrepresentation-Humanity/31275242987/bd?cm_mmc=ggl-_-UK_Shopp_Textbookstandard-_-product_id%3DUK9781844652723USED-_-keyword%3D&amp;gclid=CjwKCAjwg5uZBhATEiwAhhRLHkhJ42KsUkqh3eKRGusvrXGnNkT6hk9A7HvzeAQLBtBPiELVjW5uchoCDGgQAvD_BwE"><em>Aping Mankind: Neuromania, Darwinitis and the Misrepresentation of Humanity</em></a><em>. </em>Neuroscience ‘is often given authority where it has none’ warns Tallis.</p>
<p>Grudgingly I admit all those long words and graphs might come across well in a formal context. During a presentation to drug legislators, for example. Certainly compared to showing a clip of <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nHLpB38LNg4"><em>Tales from the Trip</em></a> on the the meeting room’s wall-mounted LCD screen. </p>
<p>So it’s sweetly satisfying for heads to beat bureaucrats at their own game with the slew of pro-psychedelic neuroscience stats flooding out of respected institutions.</p>
<p>Granted, the arcane apparatus of the psychedelic experience itself remains beyond even the grasp of they who have mastered the most multisyllabic words from this incomprehensible, in-style, inculcation. </p>
<p>“That’s still a wide open mystery,” confesses <a href="https://www.5ht2a.org">Dr Charles Nichols</a>, eminent psychedelic pharmacologist and <a href="https://www.vitalpsychedelictraining.com">Vital</a> neuroscience lecturer. </p>
<h2>“Specific pathways may be involved in the psychedelic process”</h2>
<p>Charles is the son of <a href="https://psychedelicstoday.com/2019/08/06/dr-david-nichols-a-rich-and-historical-overview-of-psychedelic-chemistry/">Dr David Nichols</a>, chemist to the stars. David made the DMT for <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-7">Rick Strassman</a>, <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-12">MDMA for MAPS</a> and <a href="https://hopkinspsychedelic.org">psilocybin for Johns Hopkins</a>.</p>
<p>“Back in the 2000s my father’s lab looked at the cross talk downstream from when G-alpha-i protein interacts with a specific beta and gamma that activates a hormone called Src, which then activates a series of enzymes. That’s the very top effector.” </p>
<p>Right. No wonder nobody’s got to the bottom of it as yet. </p>
<p>“Specific pathways may be involved,” Charles whispers conspiratorially to the cheap seats. </p>
<p>No talk of neuroscience in these pages is complete without a mention of Greatest Living Englishman Dr Robin Carhart-Harris.</p>
<p>Dr Carhart-Harris’ sympathetic yet rigorous research at scientific bastion Imperial College London brought the psychedelic experience its medical legitimacy. His <a href="https://pharmrev.aspetjournals.org/content/71/3/316">REBUS</a>, ‘relaxed beliefs under psychedelics’ model is widely considered the neatest summation of psychedelic neuroscience. (without wishing to damn it with faint praise). </p>
<h2>“My lab studies what psychedelics do that serotonin doesn’t”</h2>
<p>For anyone too embarrassed to ask the scientific way to say ‘tripping’ is ‘Relax the precision of high-level priors or beliefs, thereby liberating bottom-up information flow, particularly via intrinsic sources such as the limbic system.’ Obviously.</p>
<p>Dr Charles Nichols is a pharmacologist developing new drugs. Unlike his celebrated psychedelic chemist dad Dr David, Charles has the benefit of Carhart-Harris’ research, or its slipstream at least.</p>
<p>Charles uses his prodigious skill with the pestle, mortar and petri dish to identify, isolate and augment certain properties within his arsenal of exotic designer psychedelics.</p>
<p>Specifically, “The study is my lab now is around what psychedelics do that serotonin itself doesn’t,” says Charles. </p>
<p>Which is a lot.</p>
<p>He’s already found that mescaline-derived DOI has a tremendously positive effect on inflammatory conditions including asthma. And, he’s worked out that <em>it’s not even one of the bits that makes you trip</em>. Which has implications aplenty for widespread use. And the sensitive conversation around non-psychedelic psychedelics. </p>
<p>(<a href="https://www.lucid.news/psychedelics-inflammation-eye-disease-eleusis/">Ethnobotanist Richard Evans Schultes wrote in 1938</a>, “Some of the ills listed as responding to peyote were tuberculosis, pneumonia, scarlet fever, intestinal ills, diabetes, rheumatic pains, colds, grippe, fevers and venereal diseases.” Cheers <a href="https://www.lucid.news/psychedelics-inflammation-eye-disease-eleusis/">Mark Gunther of <em>Lucid News</em></a>).</p>
<p>Psychedelics possess less ‘inhibitory’ effects on brain receptors that might suppress ‘excitatory’ ones, compared to serotonin. They hit the accelerator while cutting the brakes: boosting neurotransmission while hindering the body’s autonomous attempts to bring body chemistry back to ‘normal’. </p>
<h2>“The profound and mystical effect itself is still a mystery”</h2>
<p>This initiates a ‘synaptic cascade’ of excitatory messages. Once that gets to the Raphe nuclei in the brain stem connected to the whole brain, it’s blast off. </p>
<p>The resulting “downstream cross talk” takes an unusual route through the nervous system. Precisely what is a little vague. Zen meditation buff Dr Bryan Roth is on it with a system he calls ‘<a href="his%20https://blog.addgene.org/understanding-gpcr-signaling-using-trupath">TRUPATH</a>, an open-source biosensor platform for interrogating the GPCR transducerome’. He’s also the guy <a href="https://newpsychonaut.com/vital-zine-16/brain-architecture-and-morality">making the non-psychedelic psychedelics for DARPA</a>.</p>
<p>“All psychedelics have a surprisingly different set of reactions with the 5-hydroxytryptamine receptors associated with serotonin,” says Charles, “but they all work on 5-hydroxytryptamine receptor 2a.” </p>
<p>Neuroscientists gave it with the catchy nickname ‘5-HT2a’. Relax, they’ve got loads more. </p>
<p>Mescaline for example only activates two other receptors besides 5-HT2a. LSD’s “complex pharmacology” on the other hand means it interacts with 17 different receptors in total. </p>
<p>‘The phrase <em>5-HT2a agonist</em> has supplanted <em>psychedelic</em>, which still carries faint whiffs of hippie-era hedonism,’ tech bible <em>Wired</em> tipped us off in its recent feature <a href="https://www.wired.com/story/race-to-engineer-new-psychedelic-drugs/"><em>The High-Stakes Race to Engineer New Psychedelic Drugs</em></a>.</p>
